‘Beneficial reuse’ of coal ash could contaminate drinking water statewide

It’s not a good sign when even the dogs won’t drink your tap water. “They sniff it and then drink the bottled water we pour,” said Frank Michna of Caledonia, one of hundreds of southeastern Wisconsin residents whose wells are contaminated by pollutants that may be coming from buried coal ash.

Endocrine Disruptors

Endocrine disruptors in pharmaceuticals, agricultural and industrial chemicals, and everyday products have been called a “global threat” to people and wildlife. In next-door Minnesota, traces have been found in nearly all the surface waters tested. But Wisconsin is lagging behind Minnesota in testing.

Drying Up

Across central Wisconsin, in a region known as the Central Sands, residents have watched water levels in lakes and small streams drop for years. In a state with about 15,000 lakes and more than a quadrillion gallons of groundwater, it is hard to believe that water could ever be in short supply. Experts say, however, that the burgeoning number of so-called high-capacity wells is drawing down some ground and surface water.
